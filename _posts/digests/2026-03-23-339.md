#### [DLSS 5 looks like a real-time generative AI filter for video games](https://www.theverge.com/news/895472/nvidia-dlss5-generative-ai-pc-graphics)
Related:
 * [Nvidia's DLSS 5 uses generative AI to boost photorealism in video games, with ambitions beyond gaming](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/16/nvidias-dlss-5-uses-generative-ai-to-boost-photo-realism-in-video-games-with-ambitions-beyond-gaming/)

![](https://platform.theverge.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/03/grokashcroft.jpg?quality=90&strip=all&crop=0%2C10.752036702561%2C100%2C78.495926594877&w=1200)

Nvidia announced **DLSS 5** at its GTC conference, introducing a major shift from previous upscaling technology by applying **generative AI** to rework lighting, materials, and character details in real time at up to **4K resolution**. Unlike earlier DLSS versions that used machine learning to bridge high and low graphics settings, DLSS 5 analyzes a single frame to understand **scene semantics** — including characters, hair, fabric, and translucent skin — alongside environmental lighting conditions, then generates new visual details like **subsurface scattering**, fabric sheen, and hair light-interaction. CEO Jensen Huang called it the "**GPT moment for graphics**," framing the fusion of structured 3D rendering data with probabilistic generative AI as a paradigm applicable far beyond gaming, potentially extending to enterprise platforms like Snowflake and Databricks. Confirmed compatible titles include **Resident Evil Requiem**, **Starfield**, **Hogwarts Legacy**, **EA Sports FC**, the **Elder Scrolls IV: Oblivion remake**, and **Assassin's Creed Shadows**, with a release scheduled for **this fall**.

Early reactions have been sharply divided, with critics like developer **Mike Bithell** calling it "nonsense" that removes artistic direction, while Bethesda's **Todd Howard** praised its effect in Starfield. Screenshots comparing DLSS 5 and original renders show character models significantly altered — protagonist Grace Ashcroft in Requiem appears to have fuller lips and heavy eyeshadow resembling an **Instagram filter**, and Starfield characters display unnatural stage-lit highlights inconsistent with their environments. Nvidia argues it honors **artistic intent** by anchoring outputs in the game's original color and motion vector data and providing developers with controls for **intensity, color grading, blending, contrast, saturation**, and gamma, as well as the ability to **mask specific objects or areas** from enhancement. Whether these controls will be sufficient to address concerns about AI overriding hand-crafted art direction remains to be seen before the fall launch.

#### [Meta's Manus Launches Desktop App With AI Agent for Tasks Across Files, Apps](https://www.pcmag.com/news/metas-manus-launches-desktop-app-with-ai-agent-for-tasks-across-files-apps)
Related:
 * [Meta's Manus launches 'My Computer' to turn your Mac into an AI agent](https://9to5mac.com/2026/03/17/metas-manus-launches-my-computer-to-turn-your-mac-into-an-ai-agent/)

![](https://i.pcmag.com/imagery/articles/01RNgHFzH2Nrh1Y830yMakA-1.fit_lim.size_1200x630.v1773827214.webp)

Manus, an AI startup acquired by Meta in December, has launched a desktop app for Mac (Apple Silicon) and Windows featuring an agentic tool called **My Computer**. Bringing Manus out of the cloud and onto local machines, the app allows the AI to manage local files, execute commands, and interact directly with apps and workflows. It operates by executing **command line instructions (CLI)** in the computer's terminal, enabling it to read, analyze, edit, and organize files. Use cases include sorting thousands of photos into categorized subfolders, batch-renaming files based on their contents, and converting file formats — tasks described as tedious but highly automatable.

Beyond basic file management, Manus can integrate with **Google apps** for remote task execution (e.g., fetching a file and emailing it to a client), build apps, use a **local GPU to train machine learning models**, and run **large language models for inference**. The app follows a permission-based model, asking users to grant folder access before automating tasks. A free tier is available, with paid plans starting at **$20/month** ($17 billed annually). The launch comes amid growing competition from similar AI agents like **OpenClaw** and **Perplexity's Personal Computer**, though experts have flagged privacy and security concerns with such tools. Notably, Chinese officials are currently reviewing the legality of Manus's acquisition by Meta, as the company was originally founded in China before relocating to Singapore.

#### [MiniMax M2.7 Testing Shows Benchmark Wins & Major Cost Savings](https://www.geeky-gadgets.com/minimax-m27-benchmark-results/)
Related:
 * [MiniMax launches M2.7 model on MiniMax Agent and APIs](https://www.testingcatalog.com/minimax-launches-m2-7-model-on-minimax-agent-and-apis/)
 * [Cursor's Composer 2 beats Opus 4.6 on coding benchmarks at a fraction of the price](https://thenewstack.io/cursors-composer-2-beats-opus-46-on-coding-benchmarks-at-a-fraction-of-the-price/)

![](https://www.geeky-gadgets.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/minimax-m27-token-pricing-comparison.jpg)

MiniMax has released its M2.7 model, available via the MiniMax Agent platform and API, featuring autonomous self-improvement through over 100 rounds of reinforcement learning and agent harnesses. Benchmark results show 56.22% on SWE-Pro (Swaybench Pro), 55.6% on VIBE-Pro, and 57% on Terminal Bench 2, while supporting 40+ complex skills with a 97% skill adherence rate and a 24K context window. Pricing starts at $0.30 per million input tokens and $120 per million output tokens, making it up to 50x cheaper than competitors like Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.6. MiniMax is also deploying M2.7 internally to automate its own R&D processes with minimal human intervention.

Meanwhile, Cursor released Composer 2, its third-generation in-house coding model trained in just five months, which scores 61.7% on Terminal-Bench 2.0, beating Opus 4.6's 58.0% though trailing GPT-5.4's 75.1%. At as little as $0.5/$2.5 per million input/output tokens versus Opus 4.6's $5/$25, Composer 2 offers a compelling price-performance ratio, with a faster default mode at $1.5/$7.5. The key technical innovation is **self-summarization**, a compaction-in-the-loop reinforcement learning technique where the model compresses its own context to ~1,000 tokens at token-length triggers, reducing compaction errors by 50%. Unlike previous Composer versions, Composer 2 also incorporates continuous pre-training before reinforcement learning, providing a stronger base model to scale RL on top of.

#### Research

[Attention Residuals](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.15031). Replacing fixed residual connections with learned attention mechanisms, this method allows each layer to selectively aggregate information from previous layers based on input content, improving model performance across different scales.

[SWE-Skills-Bench: Do Agent Skills Actually Help in Real-World Software Engineering?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.15401). This benchmark evaluates whether agent skills actually improve LLM-based agents' performance on real software engineering tasks, finding that most skills provide minimal gains while a small subset encoding specialized knowledge delivers meaningful improvements.

[V-JEPA 2.1: Unlocking Dense Features in Video Self-Supervised Learning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.14482). Using a dense predictive loss applied across all tokens in a unified image and video representation model, this approach achieves state-of-the-art results on tasks ranging from video forecasting and robot manipulation to depth estimation and action recognition.

[GradMem: Learning to Write Context into Memory with Test-Time Gradient Descent](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.13875). Language models can compress long contexts into compact memory representations by performing a few gradient descent steps on special memory tokens during inference, allowing the model to store and reuse information without reprocessing the full context.

[Delightful Policy Gradient](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.14608v1). The authors propose Delightful Policy Gradient (DPG), which gates gradient updates using a sigmoid function of advantage and action surprisal to reduce noise from unlikely actions and shift learning focus toward harder decision contexts where the policy performs worse.
